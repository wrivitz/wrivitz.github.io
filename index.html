Anti-Pluralism at The Daily Princetonian and the New Editorial Board, and Announcing a New New Editorial Board

October 1, 2017 The Princeton Board of Editorials

On the Fourteenth day of September of the Year of Our Lord 2017, the Princeton Editorial Board announced its formation in response to the structural change of the former Editorial Board at the Daily Princetonian.  As The Princeton Editorial Board stated, “Editor-in-Chief Sarah Sakha announced in a Letter from the Editor that the ‘Prince’ Editorial Board would be abolished and replaced with a group—composed of senior ‘Prince’ editors from the Managing Board and select students—that would publish opinions on certain issues ‘when exercising an institutional voice is appropriate.’” The former ‘Prince’ Editorial Board, or at least those members who did not subsequently take that paper’s offer to install each individual therein as a columnist for the section of which they used to be a part, claim this change was a logical result of the old board’s mostly conservative bent. They claimed, perhaps correctly, that their pseudo-anonymously published viewpoints had drawn censure because they were “contrary to the personal political opinions of the ‘Prince’ Editor-in-Chief and many of the senior editors.” Instead of continuing open discourse at the ‘Prince,’ they struck out on their own to form the Princeton Editorial Board.

We believe that neither the new nor old structures of the Editorial Board are necessarily flawed, but we nevertheless applaud the Princeton Editorial Board’s commitment to keeping their organization’s ideology as separate as possible from the organization who so rudely offered to keep each and every one of its members on board. As such, we here at the Princeton Board of Editorials have decided to follow their lead in preserving this campus’ pluralism, contrarian discourse, and—most importantly, of course—editorials published with the names of those penning them safely tucked out of sight. If one small group of highly opinionated Princetonians can start a group to publish opinions, why can’t we do the same?

The decision of the ‘Prince’ to abolish their Editorial Board in the middle of the calendar year has caused approximately thirteen people, of which at least ten are involved with Princeton College Republicans, to cry out, incensed and indignant. While we could in solidarity with them bemoan the internal workings of a college newspaper, we have decided to forgo that opportunity. Instead we want to reaffirm our commitment to providing a diverse and well-considered array of views from the Princeton University community. We do not particularly care about the processes or structures of the Daily Princetonian because, frankly, we have never been silenced by them. It is our position however to highlight and revisit the opinion editorial published by The Tab written in response to one of the former Editorial Board’s shining achievements regarding the Women*s Center. The response piece spicily titled, “The Daily Princetonian’s Editorial Board doesn’t want you to have sex” does a great job of what we have no intention of doing: taking the Princeton Editorial Board semi-seriously. While the dialogue was initially laughed at, then quickly forgotten, the anti-feminist rag that was “eventually published” makes up a prime piece of the persecution complex felt by the Princeton Editorial Board.

The editorial standards of the ‘Prince’ are unimportant to the foundation of The Editorial Board of Princeton. We pride ourselves on our independence, and we pride ourselves on not publishing under the masthead of an organization we have never been a part of. We are disturbed even by the concept of trying to represent ourselves under the masthead of an organization operated by people we barely talk to. There is nothing neutral in our stance here. If something is published by the Editorial Board of Princeton, we would never expect it to reflect the views the hard-working editors of the Princetonian. (With the immaculate record of copy-editing at the ‘Prince’—most recently publishing editor comments on an opinion piece in the print edition—we will gladly refrain from such tomfoolery.) We in fact harbor no grudges against the editors of the ‘Prince’ who—we are told—were so unthinkably hurtful in the disclaimers they would post on their personal Facebook accounts about the shared links to the previous works of the former Daily Princetonian Editorial Board. As the courageous decisions of the Princeton Editorial Board demonstrate, ideas cannot be suppressed—but they can be refuted, parodied, and scrutinized with every ounce of the gravity and sobriety they deserve (which, in this case, likely isn’t enough to outweigh the editorials’ weight in print).

In his remarks to the Class of 2021 during Opening Exercises, President Eisgruber refused to establish an official University stance on the Charlottesville controversy: “It is, however, neither my role nor that of the University to prescribe how you should react to this controversy or others. It is rather my role and the role of the University to encourage you to think deeply about what these events mean for this country and its core values, and to encourage you to find ways to participate constructively in the national dialogue they have generated.” We emphatically disagree with this position of deference and appeasement. We will not be the Neville Chamberlain to the Hitler that is White Supremacy.  While President Eisgruber and the Princeton Editorial Board may decline to decry Nazism and white supremacy, we here at the Princeton Board of Editorials firmly believe in pluralism and acceptance based on traits which are unchosen and immutable, not regressive beliefs that are chosen actively.

We invite all students to apply to our Board of Editorials. In our application process as well in the writing of future editorials, ideas will be evaluated by the readers. The Board will continue to publish regardless of quality. We hope that eventually, The Daily Princetonian will not follow the same model.
